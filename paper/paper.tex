\begin{abstract}
Importance sampling of minibatches can accelerate training, and the
variance-optimal proposal is classical: sample each example in proportion to its
gradient norm $\|g\|$. But $\|g\|$ conflates two very different things---examples
that are \emph{hard and learnable} and examples that are \emph{hard and
unlearnable} (noise, mislabelled or corrupted data). Both have large gradients,
so a gradient-norm sampler preferentially feeds the model its noisiest data. We
argue the quantity worth targeting is not gradient magnitude but \emph{reducible
improvement}---how much training on an input actually lowers loss on data one
cares about---and that a small network can be trained, online, to estimate it and
amortise it as a learned data scorer. We build to this in stages. As the
substrate we introduce a learned proposal $q_\phi$: a small neural network that
maps the candidate batch---through cheap, forward-pass byproduct features (loss, a
logit-gradient-norm proxy, predictive entropy, confidence)---to a change of
measure over the batch. It is trained by a \emph{stable convex} cross-entropy
meta-objective (the natural variance-minimising REINFORCE update diverges, and we
explain why), and \emph{periodically refreshed}---meta-trained in short bursts and
otherwise used from cheap features alone---so that the expensive target need only
be computed on $\sim\!10\%$ of steps. With an importance-weighted, \emph{unbiased}
update this matches the every-step gradient-norm oracle while removing $90\%$ of
its selection passes; \emph{dropping} the debiasing weights yields a faster biased
hard-example-mining variant. The headline is the final stage: a \emph{biased
reducible-improvement sampler}, trained on measured held-out improvement rather
than gradient norm, concentrates on the learnable tier and \emph{ignores the
noise the gradient-norm sampler actively chases}, reaching the target capability
$\sim\!4.2\times$ faster than uniform and $\sim\!1.9\times$ faster than the
gradient-norm sampler---which, by chasing noise, ends no better than uniform. We
give the unbiasedness argument, the stability analysis, and a fully
self-contained NumPy implementation with an evaluation harness, and position the
method as the stochastic-optimisation analogue of a learned Girsanov change of
measure used for variance reduction in path-dependent Monte Carlo
\citep{virrion2020}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Stochastic gradient descent estimates the full-dataset gradient
$\bar g = \tfrac1N \sum_{i=1}^N g_i$ by an average over a uniformly sampled
minibatch. The uniform estimator is unbiased but not variance-minimal: examples
contribute very unequally to the gradient, and a sampler that concentrates on
high-contribution examples---while reweighting to remain unbiased---produces a
lower-variance estimate of $\bar g$ for the same number of backward passes
\citep{alain2015,katharopoulos2018}. The variance-optimal proposal is classical:
sample example $i$ with probability proportional to $\|g_i\|$.

The difficulty is operational. Evaluating $\|g_i\|$ for every candidate example
at every step costs roughly as much as the training it is meant to accelerate.
The standard response is a heuristic: approximate $\|g_i\|$ by a cheap proxy
(e.g.\ the gradient norm with respect to the logits) computed on a candidate
pool each step. This still pays a per-step pool cost, and it is \emph{fixed}: it
cannot adapt to the model's changing notion of which examples are informative,
and it cannot be reused.

\paragraph{From gradient norm to reducible improvement.} There is a deeper
problem with the classical target than its cost. Sampling $\propto\|g_i\|$ is
variance-optimal, but the gradient norm conflates examples that are \emph{hard and
learnable} with examples that are \emph{hard and unlearnable}---noise, mislabelled
or corrupted data, ubiquitous in web-scale corpora. Both have large gradients, yet
only the first is worth training on, so a gradient-norm sampler preferentially
feeds the model its noisiest data. The quantity actually worth targeting is not
gradient magnitude but \emph{reducible} improvement: how much training on an input
lowers loss on data one cares about. This reframing---from ``where is the gradient
large'' to ``where is learning possible''---is the conceptual centre of this paper,
and it turns out a small network can be trained online to estimate it.

\paragraph{Contribution.} We develop a \emph{learned}, neural sampling proposal
$q_\phi$ over cheap features and build, in stages, from variance reduction to
reducible-improvement sampling:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Learned proposal and unbiasedness (substrate).} We formulate the
learned reweighter---a small network mapping the candidate batch to a change of
measure over it---and prove the importance-weighted update it induces is unbiased
for any strictly positive proposal (Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased}), so it can
only change variance, not the expected trajectory.
\item \textbf{A stable meta-objective.} We show the natural ``minimise the
estimator variance directly by REINFORCE'' update is numerically unstable---the
per-sample cost spans many orders of magnitude and $\phi$ diverges in a feedback
loop with the proposal---and replace it with a convex distribution-matching
objective ($\mathrm{KL}(p^\star \,\|\, q_\phi)$) with the same optimum and
well-behaved dynamics.
\item \textbf{Amortisation by periodic refresh.} The reweighter is meta-trained
in short bursts and otherwise used from cheap features alone, so the expensive
gradient-norm target is computed on only $\sim\!10\%$ of steps---a $90\%$
reduction in selection passes---while the unbiased variant still matches the
every-step oracle and beats uniform.
\item \textbf{Biased sampling for rare, learnable pain.} Dropping the debiasing
weights turns the same change of measure into soft hard-example mining: faster
where the informative examples are a rare minority (up to $5.4\times$), at the
price of bias and a narrower stable learning-rate range.
\item \textbf{Reducible-improvement sampling (headline).} We train the scorer on
\emph{realized held-out improvement} rather than gradient magnitude. It learns to
concentrate on the learnable tier and ignore unlearnable noise---the noise a
gradient-norm sampler actively chases---reaching the target capability
$\sim\!4.2\times$ faster than uniform and $\sim\!1.9\times$ faster than the
gradient-norm sampler, which by chasing noise ends no better than uniform.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Relation to a learned change of measure.} The construction is the
discrete-optimisation counterpart of adaptive importance sampling for
path-dependent Monte Carlo, in which a neural network parametrises a Girsanov
drift that reorients sampled trajectories toward the region where the integrand
(the payoff) has mass, trained to minimise estimator variance and reused across
many pricings \citep{virrion2020}. The parallel is structural: there a neural
network maps the past of a trajectory to a change of measure on path space; here
a neural network maps the candidate batch (its per-example features) to a
discrete change of measure over the batch. In both cases the change of measure
leaves the target expectation invariant by construction and is optimised to steer
sampling toward the region that matters. The one honest qualifier is the training
signal: rather than minimising estimator variance directly (which we show is
unstable here, \S\ref{ssec:meta}), we train the network to reproduce an explicit
target through a convex surrogate---initially the variance-optimal
$p^\star\propto\|g\|$, and ultimately the reducible-improvement target that
distinguishes learnable from unlearnable data. The shared principle is:
\emph{learn a sampling distribution that concentrates effort where it most helps
the objective, while reweighting (or knowingly declining to) to control the
expectation.}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Importance sampling and variance reduction for SGD.}
That non-uniform minibatch sampling can reduce the variance of the stochastic
gradient---and that the variance-optimal proposal samples example $i$ in
proportion to its gradient norm $\|g_i\|$---is by now classical.
\citet{needell2014} and \citet{zhao2015} establish the result and its convergence
consequences for convex objectives, and \citet{csiba2018} extend it to minibatch
proposals. For deep networks, where exact per-example gradient norms are
prohibitive, a literature of cheap surrogates has grown:
\citet{alain2015} sample in proportion to gradient norms in a distributed setup;
\citet{katharopoulos2017} use a biased upper bound and \citet{katharopoulos2018}
an upper bound on the gradient norm computed from the loss layer alone;
\citet{johnson2018} maintain a robust, periodically-corrected approximation
(RAIS); and \citet{stich2017} give a \emph{safe} adaptive scheme that never does
worse than uniform. \citet{faghri2020} study how gradient variance evolves during
training and when importance sampling actually helps. Our cheap features (loss,
a logit-gradient-norm proxy, entropy, confidence) are in this surrogate family;
the novelty is that we do not commit to a \emph{fixed} surrogate but \emph{learn}
a proposal over them and amortise it across steps.

\paragraph{Online batch selection, curriculum, and hard-example mining.}
A parallel line reweights or reorders examples by a notion of difficulty rather
than by gradient-variance optimality. Curriculum learning \citep{bengio2009} and
self-paced learning \citep{kumar2010} schedule easy-to-hard exposure; online
batch selection \citep{loshchilov2015} prioritises high-loss examples; hard-example
mining \citep{shrivastava2016} and the focal loss \citep{lin2017} up-weight hard
examples in detection; and prioritized experience replay \citep{schaul2015} is the
reinforcement-learning analogue, replaying high-error transitions with
importance-weight correction---exactly the bias/variance bookkeeping we use here.
Closest in spirit are methods that \emph{learn} the selection rule:
\citet{jiang2018} train a ``MentorNet'' to weight examples, and \citet{fan2018}
cast data selection as a learned ``teacher'' policy. We share the learned-selector
idea but target the variance-optimal proposal with a convex, stable meta-objective
and amortise it by periodic refresh, rather than learning an open-ended difficulty
heuristic.

\paragraph{Learnability- and influence-based selection.}
A distinct line argues that the right thing to prioritise is not difficulty or
gradient magnitude but how much an example will actually \emph{reduce} the loss one
cares about, precisely because high loss (or high gradient) can come from
irreducible noise. RHO-LOSS \citep{mindermann2022} selects points that are
learnable, worth learning, and not yet learnt, using a held-out reference model to
estimate reducible loss. The notion of an example's realized effect on a target
objective is made precise by influence functions \citep{koh2017} and by tracing
gradient descent (TracIn) \citep{pruthi2020}, and learned data weighting appears in
robust example reweighting \citep{ren2018} and in domain reweighting for language
model pretraining (DoReMi) \citep{xie2023}. Our reducible-improvement sampler
(\S\ref{ssec:reducible}) sits squarely in this line: it trains the proposal network
to estimate \emph{measured} held-out improvement online and amortise it as a learned
data scorer, and we show directly that this sidesteps the gradient-norm sampler's
failure mode of chasing unlearnable noise.

\paragraph{Adaptive and neural importance sampling for Monte Carlo.}
Outside optimisation, adaptively learning a change of measure to reduce
estimator variance is a mature subject: the cross-entropy method
\citep{rubinstein2004} fits a parametric sampler by minimising KL to the
zero-variance target---structurally the same move as our $\mathrm{KL}(p^\star\,\|\,
q_\phi)$ meta-objective---and adaptive importance sampling more broadly is
surveyed by \citet{bugallo2017} (see also \citet{owen2013} for the underlying
theory). Recent work parametrises the proposal with neural networks:
\citet{mueller2019} learn an importance sampler with normalizing flows for
rendering, and \citet{virrion2020} learns a Girsanov drift network for
path-dependent option pricing. Our construction is the discrete-optimisation
counterpart of the latter: a network maps a batch to a change of measure over the
pool, trained purely for variance while leaving the target expectation invariant.

\paragraph{Score-function estimators.}
Our stability analysis (\S\ref{ssec:meta}) concerns the score-function /
REINFORCE estimator \citep{williams1992}, whose notoriously high variance has
driven a large body of control-variate and baseline techniques
\citep{mnih2014}. We sidestep it entirely by replacing the
variance-of-the-variance objective with a convex distribution-matching surrogate
that has the same optimum.

\section{Method}

\subsection{Setup and unbiasedness}
Let the candidate pool at a step be examples $\{1,\dots,n\}$ with per-example
gradients $g_i$. Draw a minibatch of size $B$ by sampling indices i.i.d.\ from a
proposal $p=(p_1,\dots,p_n)$, $p_i>0$. Define the importance-weighted estimator
\begin{equation}
\hat g \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{1}{n\,p_{i_b}}\, g_{i_b}.
\label{eq:est}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:unbiased}
For any proposal with $p_i>0$ for all $i$, $\;\mathbb{E}[\hat g] = \bar g
= \tfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^n g_i$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each term has expectation
$\mathbb{E}_{i\sim p}\!\left[\frac{1}{n p_i} g_i\right]
= \sum_{i=1}^n p_i \frac{1}{n p_i} g_i = \frac1n \sum_i g_i = \bar g$, and the
average of $B$ i.i.d.\ unbiased terms is unbiased.
\end{proof}

Thus the choice of $p$ affects only $\mathrm{Var}(\hat g)$. The total-variance
minimiser among all proposals is $p^\star_i \propto \|g_i\|$
\citep{alain2015,katharopoulos2018}; we take that as the target the reweighter
should reproduce from cheap features.

\subsection{The learned reweighter}
We attach to each pool example a feature vector $x_i\in\mathbb{R}^F$ whose entries
are byproducts of the forward pass already required to sample: the current loss,
a logit-gradient-norm proxy $\|\partial \mathcal L_i/\partial \text{logits}\|$,
the predictive entropy, the max predicted probability, and a bias term. The
reweighter is a small neural network---a three-hidden-layer MLP shared across
pool examples, followed by a softmax over the pool,
\begin{align}
h_i^{(1)} &= \tanh(W_1^\top x_i + b_1), \qquad
h_i^{(\ell)} = \tanh(W_\ell^\top h_i^{(\ell-1)} + b_\ell)\ \ (\ell=2,3), \nonumber\\
s_i &= W_4^\top h_i^{(3)} + b_4, \qquad
q_\phi(x)_i = \frac{\exp(s_i / \tau)}{\sum_{j}\exp(s_j/\tau)},
\end{align}
with parameters $\phi=(W_1,b_1,\dots,W_4,b_4)$. The network maps the batch (its
feature matrix) to a probability measure over the pool---the importance-sampling
change of measure---mirroring the Girsanov-drift network of \citet{virrion2020}.
It can represent $p^\star$ exactly when one feature equals $\|g_i\|$, and the
stacked hidden layers let it capture the graded difficulty structure of a
heterogeneous task that a linear score or fixed proxy cannot. We mix in a small
uniform floor, $p = (1-\epsilon)q_\phi + \epsilon/n$, to guarantee $p_i>0$ and
keep weights finite.

\subsection{A stable meta-objective}\label{ssec:meta}
\paragraph{What fails.} The direct approach minimises the estimator variance
proxy $\mathbb{E}_{i\sim p}[C(i)]$ with
$C(i)=\big(\tfrac{1}{n p_i}\big)^2\|g_i\|^2$ by REINFORCE,
$\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}[C] = \mathbb{E}[\,C(i)\,\nabla_\phi \log p_i\,]$. In
practice $C$ ranges over many orders of magnitude; the score-function gradient is
dominated by rare huge-cost samples, $\phi$ overshoots, the proposal collapses,
the importance weights $1/(n p_i)$ explode, and $C$ grows further---a divergent
feedback loop. We observed exactly this: $\phi$ grew to $\sim\!10^6$ and the
validation loss diverged.

\paragraph{What works.} Because the optimum is known to be $p^\star\propto\|g\|$,
we fit $q_\phi$ to $p^\star$ directly by minimising
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\text{meta}}(\phi) = \mathrm{KL}\!\left(p^\star \,\big\|\, q_\phi\right)
= -\sum_i p^\star_i \log q_\phi(x)_i + \text{const}.
\end{equation}
For a softmax over per-example scores $s_i$, the gradient with respect to the
scores is the standard cross-entropy form
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \mathcal L_{\text{meta}}}{\partial s_i}
= q_\phi(x)_i - p^\star_i ,
\label{eq:metagrad}
\end{equation}
which we backpropagate through the network to obtain $\nabla_\phi\mathcal
L_{\text{meta}}$. This signal is well scaled (both $q$ and $p^\star$ are
probability vectors) and convex in the score logits; we additionally
trust-region the step by capping the global gradient norm. The
target $p^\star$ still uses gradient norms, so a meta-training burst pays the pool
cost---but only during the brief, intermittent refresh phases.

\subsection{Amortisation by periodic refresh}
Rather than freeze the network once, we amortise it with a duty-cycle. In each
period of $R$ steps we meta-train for a short burst of $T_b\ll R$ steps---paying
the gradient-norm pool cost and updating $\phi$---and for the remaining $R-T_b$
steps we use the network frozen, sampling from the cheap features alone with
\emph{no} gradient-norm pass while still drawing an importance-sampled, debiased
minibatch. Each refresh is warm-started from the previous $\phi$, so the proposal
re-adapts to the model's drifting notion of difficulty at negligible cost instead
of going stale. Over a run this computes the expensive target on a $T_b/R$
fraction of steps. This is where the learned proposal earns its keep relative to
the fixed heuristic, which must recompute its proxy \emph{every} step.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Training with a learned, periodically-refreshed reweighter network}
\label{alg:main}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{input:} model $\theta$, reweighter network $\phi$, pool size $n$,
batch $B$, lr $\eta$, meta-lr $\beta$, refresh period $R$, burst length $T_b$
\For{$t=1,2,\dots$}
  \State sample candidate pool of $n$ examples
  \State forward pass on pool; form features $x_i$ and proposal $q_\phi(x)$
  \State $\textsc{meta} \gets [\,(t \bmod R) < T_b\,]$ \hfill\Comment{in a refresh burst?}
  \If{\textsc{meta}} compute $\|g_i\|$ on pool; \quad $p^\star_i \gets \|g_i\|/\sum_j\|g_j\|$ \EndIf
  \State sample minibatch indices $i_1,\dots,i_B \sim p=(1-\epsilon)q_\phi+\epsilon/n$
  \State weights $w_b \gets 1/(n\,p_{i_b})$, clip and normalise to mean $1$
  \State $\theta \gets \theta - \eta\, \tfrac1B\sum_b w_b\, g_{i_b}$
  \hfill\Comment{unbiased weighted update}
  \If{\textsc{meta}} set $\partial\mathcal L/\partial s_i \gets q_\phi(x)_i - p^\star_i$;
  \ backprop through the network; \ $\phi \gets \phi - \beta\,\nabla_\phi\mathcal L$
  \hfill\Comment{convex meta-step}\EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Task.} We train a fixed-context char-level MLP language model
(context $8$, embedding $24$, hidden $128$, $\tanh$) by SGD on a synthetic corpus
with deliberately heterogeneous difficulty. To make the difficulty
\emph{graded}---a continuum rather than an easy/hard dichotomy---and the task
globally harder, the corpus mixes three tiers: $45\%$ deterministic template
phrases (easy, low gradient); $35\%$ stochastic-slot phrases, fixed skeletons
whose subject/verb/object/place words are drawn at random from small pools, so
context only partly predicts the next character (medium, intermediate gradient);
and $20\%$ longer high-entropy alphanumeric bursts (hard, high gradient). The
middle tier populates the difficulty spectrum between trivially-easy and
near-random, and the reduced easy fraction means the task does not saturate
within the training budget. This is the regime where importance sampling has the
most to exploit; under fully homogeneous data, uniform sampling is already
near-optimal and no method can help.

\paragraph{Conditions.} All three are matched on the compute budget (gradient
examples consumed $=$ steps $\times$ batch):
\emph{(i) Uniform} standard SGD;
\emph{(ii) Grad-norm IS}, the fixed heuristic, sampling the minibatch from the
pool with $p\propto\|g\|$ proxy every step;
\emph{(iii) Learned (ours)}, Algorithm~\ref{alg:main}, a neural reweighter on a
periodic-refresh schedule (burst $T_b=100$, period $R=1000$, over $4000$ steps),
so it meta-trains during only $4\times100=400$ steps and is otherwise frozen.
We report mean $\pm$ s.d.\ over $5$ seeds.

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figure~\ref{fig:curves} summarise
the outcome. Unlike the saturating task of earlier experiments, this harder
corpus does \emph{not} bottom out within the $4000$-step budget, so the benefit
of importance sampling now persists all the way to the final loss. The learned
reweighter attains the lowest final validation loss, $0.521\pm0.035$, against
$0.526\pm0.031$ for the fixed heuristic and $0.533\pm0.036$ for uniform SGD. The
improvement over uniform is statistically significant (paired over 5 seeds,
$\Delta=0.011$, $t=3.66$), while the gap to the every-step heuristic is within
noise ($\Delta=0.005$, $t=2.42$): the learned proposal \emph{matches} the oracle
heuristic and beats uniform. The same ordering shows up in sample efficiency:
both importance samplers reach a target loss of $0.553$ with roughly $18$--$20\%$
fewer gradient examples than uniform ($194$k and $199$k vs $243$k;
Table~\ref{tab:main}, second row). Figure~\ref{fig:phi} shows the learned network
recovered the \emph{right} signal: of the input features, the gradient-norm proxy
has the largest effect (saliency) on the network's proposal score---with
predictive entropy close behind---i.e.\ it recovers $p^\star\propto\|g\|$ from
cheap features rather than being told, while also leaning on correlated
uncertainty cues. Crucially (Table~\ref{tab:overhead}), it does so while computing
the expensive per-example gradient-norm target on only $400$ steps instead of
$4000$---a $90\%$ reduction in selection overhead---because outside the short
refresh bursts it samples from cheap features alone.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} On this harder, non-saturating task the learned
method does more than break even: it reaches a lower final loss than uniform SGD
(significantly so) and statistically matches the every-step gradient-norm oracle.
But the headline is not ``the learned method beats the heuristic''---the gap to
the heuristic is within noise, and the heuristic is an every-step oracle. It is
that \emph{a cheap, reusable network reproduces the oracle's benefit at a fraction
of its selection cost}: it reaches the target loss about as fast as the oracle,
and the same final loss, while computing the expensive gradient-norm target on
only one step in ten. That is the
property that matters when the per-example gradient computation is the bottleneck
and when one wants a proposal that can be reused (across steps, and in principle
across runs or models). This mirrors the ``train once, reuse for a week''
robustness of the learned Girsanov sampler in the Monte Carlo setting
\citep{virrion2020}.

\subsection{Concentrated difficulty: when the pain is rare}\label{ssec:concentrated}
The graded task above spreads difficulty across a continuum. We now stress the
opposite, more extreme regime---one the method is designed for---in which the
informative ``pain'' is a \emph{tiny} fraction of the data. We build a second
synthetic corpus in which $99\%$ of the text is a handful of trivially
predictable deterministic phrases (built from common letters only, so they are
mastered almost immediately) and the remaining $\sim\!1\%$ is a \emph{rare-but-
learnable} hard tier: a small fixed codebook of deterministic phrases written in
rare letters ($\{j,k,q,v,w,x,z\}$) that never appear in the easy text. The hard
tier is learnable (deterministic, so its loss is reducible) but data-hungry, and
the model, conditions, and budget are otherwise unchanged.

\paragraph{Why a hard-subset metric.} Because $99\%$ of the data is trivial, it
dominates the aggregate validation loss and dilutes any difference roughly
$100$-fold: indeed all three conditions finish at the same \emph{overall} loss
($\approx\!0.092$ nats/char). The benefit is entirely concentrated in the rare
tier, so we measure the loss on the hard $1\%$ subset and report how many more
training examples uniform SGD needs to reach the same hard-subset loss as the
importance samplers.

\paragraph{Result.} Importance sampling does not change the converged optimum
(the update is unbiased, Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased}); its benefit is reduced
gradient-estimate variance, which on this concentrated task manifests as a
\emph{sample-efficiency} gain---uniform SGD travels the same path but needs more

and here the learned reweighter \emph{matches} the every-step oracle ($t=0.2$,
within noise) while still computing the gradient-norm target on only $10\%$ of
steps (Figure~\ref{fig:concentrated}). This is the regime where concentrating
the sampling effort pays off most.

\subsection{Trading unbiasedness for speed: biased sampling}\label{ssec:biased}
Everything so far keeps the importance-weighted update \emph{unbiased}
(Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased}), so the change of measure can only reduce
variance, never steer the optimisation. In the concentrated regime that caps the
benefit at the variance-reduction speed-up of $\sim\!1.5\times$ above. We now ask
what happens if we keep the change of measure for \emph{sampling}---draw the
minibatch from the pool in proportion to the gradient-norm proxy (or the learned
proposal)---but \emph{drop} the importance weights and train on the ordinary
plain-mean gradient. The resulting update is deliberately \emph{biased}: it
oversamples high-gradient examples and does not correct for it, steering the
trajectory toward the rare hard tier (soft hard-example mining).

\paragraph{Result.} On the concentrated task this is dramatically faster
(Table~\ref{tab:biased}, Figure~\ref{fig:biased}). To reach a hard-subset loss of
$0.9$, biased grad-norm sampling needs $5.4\times$ fewer gradient examples than
uniform---and $\sim\!3.6\times$ fewer than the \emph{unbiased} grad-norm
sampler---while also reaching the lowest final hard-subset loss
($0.37\pm0.04$ vs $0.84\pm0.18$ for uniform; paired $t=5.3$ over 5 seeds). The
learned proposal behaves the same way ($\sim\!4\times$ faster than uniform). The
aggregate loss is unaffected ($\approx\!0.094$), so the easy majority is not
sacrificed; the gain is concentrated entirely on the rare hard tier.

\paragraph{The two costs.} This speed is bought with two concessions, and we
report both. \emph{(i) Bias.} The update no longer targets the true objective: it
optimises a gradient-norm-tilted surrogate, which helps only when the
high-gradient examples are exactly the ones one cares about. In the spread-out
graded task of our main experiment the same biased update instead
\emph{increased} final validation loss (to $0.61$ vs $0.55$ for uniform, 3 seeds),
because there much of the gradient mass sits on the near-random, partly
irreducible bursts. \emph{(ii) Stability.} Removing the debiasing weights makes
the update markedly less robust to the learning rate. Table~\ref{tab:biaslr}: at
the rate used throughout ($\eta=0.5$) all methods are stable, but the biased
samplers \emph{diverge} for every $\eta\ge1.0$ (3/3 seeds), whereas uniform SGD
(and the unbiased samplers) remain stable up to $\eta=3.0$. The biased variant
therefore demands a conservatively small step size. We read biased sampling as the
aggressive complement to the unbiased reweighter: far faster where the pain is
rare and learnable, at the price of unbiasedness and a narrower stable-rate range.

\subsection{Learning what remains to be learned: reducible-improvement sampling}
\label{ssec:reducible}
Sampling proportional to the gradient norm is variance-optimal, but gradient norm
conflates two very different things: examples that are \emph{hard and learnable}
and examples that are \emph{hard and unlearnable} (noise, mislabelled or corrupted
data---ubiquitous in web-scale corpora). Both have large gradients, yet only the
first is worth training on; a gradient-norm sampler will preferentially feed the
model its noisiest data. The right target is not gradient magnitude but
\emph{reducible} improvement---how much training on an input actually lowers loss
on data one cares about \citep{mindermann2022}, i.e.\ its realized influence on a
held-out objective \citep{koh2017,pruthi2020}. Here we test whether the sampler
network can be trained to estimate that quantity directly, rather than the
gradient, and amortise it as a learned data scorer (in the spirit of learned
example reweighting \citep{ren2018} and domain reweighting \citep{xie2023}).

\paragraph{Setup.} We build a corpus where high gradient does \emph{not} imply
learnable: $90\%$ trivially-easy text, plus two high-gradient tiers in disjoint
content regions---a \emph{learnable} tier (a fixed deterministic codebook in one
rare-letter set, reducible) and a \emph{noise} tier (freshly-random tokens in a
disjoint rare-letter set, irreducible). During short periodic bursts we measure
realized improvement by \emph{route 2}: cluster the candidate pool by content,
take one gradient step on each cluster, and record the reduction it produces in
loss on a held-out reference set of learnable examples,
$\Delta_g = L_{\text{ref}}(\theta) - L_{\text{ref}}(\theta - \alpha\,\bar g_g)$.
(Per-\emph{example} realized improvement is far too noisy to use as a label---a
single step's effect is dominated by variance---so we measure at cluster
granularity, which denoises it at a cost of only a few reference evaluations per
burst.) The scorer is given content-aware features (the mean context embedding, so
it can tell input regions apart) and meta-trained to a proposal
$\propto\max(\Delta,0)$, so unlearnable clusters get $\sim\!0$ weight. We sample
from the scorer and use the biased plain-mean update of \S\ref{ssec:biased}.

\paragraph{Result.} The two samplers target opposite things
(Figure~\ref{fig:redweights}): the gradient-norm sampler puts its \emph{largest}
weight on the unlearnable noise ($2.5\times$ uniform) and under-samples the
learnable tier ($0.3\times$), while the reducible-improvement scorer concentrates
on the learnable tier ($1.6\times$) and leaves noise near uniform ($0.9\times$)---it
learned, from measured improvement, to ignore the junk that the gradient norm
cannot distinguish. On the capability we care about (Table~\ref{tab:reducible},
Figure~\ref{fig:reducible}) this is decisive: the biased reducible sampler reaches
the learnable-subset loss \emph{$4.2$--$4.3\times$ faster than uniform} and
$\sim\!1.9\times$ faster than the gradient-norm sampler, and attains the lowest
final learnable loss ($0.260$ vs $0.291$ uniform). The gradient-norm sampler, by
chasing noise, actually ends up \emph{no better than uniform} on the learnable
capability ($0.305$) despite an early speed-up---a concrete instance of the
failure mode that motivates reducible-loss selection.

\paragraph{Biased vs.\ unbiased.} The speed-up above uses the biased plain-mean
update. If we instead \emph{debias}---use the same learned change of measure as an
importance-sampling proposal with weights $1/(n p_i)$, so the estimator is unbiased
for the full-pool gradient---the speed-up essentially vanishes: the unbiased
reducible sampler falls back to roughly uniform ($0.292$ final loss,
$\sim\!0.9\times$ uniform's examples-to-target; Table~\ref{tab:reducible}). This is
expected---an unbiased estimator shares uniform's \emph{expected} trajectory for
any proposal (Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased})---so the gain was intrinsically a
property of the \emph{biased} objective, which concentrates learning on the
learnable tier rather than fitting the full (noise-laden) gradient. What debiasing
buys instead is robustness: in a learning-rate sweep the unbiased variant remains
stable at $\eta=2.0$, where the biased variant begins to diverge, so debiasing
trades the speed-up for a wider stable-rate range. (Unbiased gradnorm-IS does not
even match uniform here---its $\propto\|g\|$ proposal pours weight on the
high-gradient noise, and the resulting importance weights add variance.)

\paragraph{Costs.} The signal is more expensive than a gradient-norm pass: each
burst pays a forward and backward pass plus a reference evaluation per content
cluster, and it requires a held-out reference set that \emph{defines} the target
capability (choose it wrongly and the scorer optimises the wrong objective).
Within those costs, it demonstrates the point: a sampler trained on \emph{realized
improvement} rather than gradient magnitude focuses compute where learning is
actually possible.

\section{Limitations}
The experiment is deliberately small and controlled (a char-level MLP on
synthetic data, CPU-only, pure NumPy) to make the mechanism and the
stability analysis transparent and fully reproducible; we do not claim
wall-clock gains at scale. The gradient-norm proxy is the logit-space norm rather
than the full parameter-gradient norm. The reweighter is a three-hidden-layer MLP
over a handful of features; richer features or a still larger network may capture
difficulty structure that the fixed heuristic misses, which is the most promising
route to \emph{exceeding} rather than merely approximating the heuristic. Finally, the
frozen phases between refreshes trade a small amount of final accuracy for the
overhead saving; the burst length $T_b$ and refresh period $R$ are tunable knobs
on that trade-off (shorter, rarer bursts save more compute but let the proposal
drift further from $p^\star$ between refreshes).

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a learned, amortised importance-reweighting scheme for SGD that
replaces a fixed gradient-norm heuristic with a small proposal network trained by
a stable, convex distribution-matching meta-objective. It preserves unbiasedness
exactly, recovers the variance-optimal signal from cheap features, and removes
$90\%$ of the expensive selection passes by reusing the proposal between periodic
refreshes.
The viewpoint---learn a sampling distribution that concentrates effort where the
integrand is large while reweighting to preserve the expectation---ports directly
from adaptive importance sampling for path-dependent Monte Carlo
\citep{virrion2020} to stochastic gradient training.
