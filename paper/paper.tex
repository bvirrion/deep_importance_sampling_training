\title{\textbf{Learned Importance Reweighting for Stochastic Gradient
Training:\\ Amortising the Variance-Optimal Proposal}}
\author{}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Importance sampling of minibatches can reduce the variance of the stochastic
gradient estimator and thereby accelerate training, but the variance-optimal
proposal---sampling each example in proportion to its gradient norm---requires a
per-example gradient computation over a candidate pool at \emph{every} step,
which is precisely the cost one is trying to save. We study replacing this fixed
heuristic with a small \emph{learned reweighting function} $q_\phi$ that maps
cheap, forward-pass byproduct features (current loss, a logit-gradient-norm
proxy, predictive entropy, confidence) to a sampling proposal. The reweighter is
trained online by matching its proposal to the variance-optimal distribution
through a convex cross-entropy meta-objective, and is then \emph{frozen and
amortised}: it continues to supply an importance-sampling proposal from cheap
features without recomputing gradient norms. On a controlled char-level language
modelling task with heterogeneous example difficulty, the learned reweighter
recovers most of the benefit of the oracle gradient-norm heuristic while
eliminating $75\%$ of the expensive per-example gradient-norm passes. We give the
unbiasedness argument for the importance-weighted update, describe a stabilised
meta-update (an earlier raw variance-minimising REINFORCE update diverges, and we
explain why), and release a fully self-contained NumPy implementation with an
A/B/C evaluation harness. We position the method as the stochastic-optimisation
analogue of a learned Girsanov change of measure used for variance reduction in
path-dependent Monte Carlo \citep{virrion2020}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Stochastic gradient descent estimates the full-dataset gradient
$\bar g = \tfrac1N \sum_{i=1}^N g_i$ by an average over a uniformly sampled
minibatch. The uniform estimator is unbiased but not variance-minimal: examples
contribute very unequally to the gradient, and a sampler that concentrates on
high-contribution examples---while reweighting to remain unbiased---produces a
lower-variance estimate of $\bar g$ for the same number of backward passes
\citep{alain2015,katharopoulos2018}. The variance-optimal proposal is classical:
sample example $i$ with probability proportional to $\|g_i\|$.

The difficulty is operational. Evaluating $\|g_i\|$ for every candidate example
at every step costs roughly as much as the training it is meant to accelerate.
The standard response is a heuristic: approximate $\|g_i\|$ by a cheap proxy
(e.g.\ the gradient norm with respect to the logits) computed on a candidate
pool each step. This still pays a per-step pool cost, and it is \emph{fixed}: it
cannot adapt to the model's changing notion of which examples are informative,
and it cannot be reused.

\paragraph{Contribution.} We replace the fixed heuristic with a \emph{learned}
proposal $q_\phi$ over cheap features, trained so that sampling from it reduces
estimator variance, and \emph{amortised} so that the expensive gradient-norm
target need only be computed during an initial meta-training phase. Concretely:
\begin{enumerate}
\item We formulate the learned reweighter and prove the importance-weighted
update it induces is unbiased for any strictly positive proposal
(Proposition~\ref{prop:unbiased}), so the method can only change variance, not
the expected optimisation trajectory.
\item We give a \emph{stable} meta-objective. We first show that the natural
``minimise the estimator variance directly by REINFORCE'' update is numerically
unstable---the per-sample cost spans many orders of magnitude and $\phi$ diverges
in a feedback loop with the proposal---and replace it with a convex
distribution-matching objective ($\mathrm{KL}(p^\star \,\|\, q_\phi)$) that has
the same optimum and well-behaved dynamics.
\item We demonstrate amortisation: after freezing the reweighter, training
proceeds with importance sampling from cheap features alone, removing $75\%$ of
the gradient-norm passes while retaining most of the benefit.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Relation to a learned change of measure.} The construction is the
discrete-optimisation counterpart of adaptive importance sampling for
path-dependent Monte Carlo, in which a neural network parametrises a Girsanov
drift that reorients sampled trajectories toward the region where the integrand
(the payoff) has mass, trained to minimise estimator variance and reused across
many pricings \citep{virrion2020}. There, the change of measure leaves the target
expectation invariant by construction and is optimised purely for variance;
here, the importance weights play the same role for the gradient estimator. The
shared principle is: \emph{learn a sampling distribution that concentrates effort
where the integrand is large, while reweighting to preserve the expectation.}

\section{Method}

\subsection{Setup and unbiasedness}
Let the candidate pool at a step be examples $\{1,\dots,n\}$ with per-example
gradients $g_i$. Draw a minibatch of size $B$ by sampling indices i.i.d.\ from a
proposal $p=(p_1,\dots,p_n)$, $p_i>0$. Define the importance-weighted estimator
\begin{equation}
\hat g \;=\; \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^{B} \frac{1}{n\,p_{i_b}}\, g_{i_b}.
\label{eq:est}
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:unbiased}
For any proposal with $p_i>0$ for all $i$, $\;\mathbb{E}[\hat g] = \bar g
= \tfrac1n\sum_{i=1}^n g_i$.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
Each term has expectation
$\mathbb{E}_{i\sim p}\!\left[\frac{1}{n p_i} g_i\right]
= \sum_{i=1}^n p_i \frac{1}{n p_i} g_i = \frac1n \sum_i g_i = \bar g$, and the
average of $B$ i.i.d.\ unbiased terms is unbiased.
\end{proof}

Thus the choice of $p$ affects only $\mathrm{Var}(\hat g)$. The total-variance
minimiser among all proposals is $p^\star_i \propto \|g_i\|$
\citep{alain2015,katharopoulos2018}; we take that as the target the reweighter
should reproduce from cheap features.

\subsection{The learned reweighter}
We attach to each pool example a feature vector $x_i\in\mathbb{R}^F$ whose entries
are byproducts of the forward pass already required to sample: the current loss,
a logit-gradient-norm proxy $\|\partial \mathcal L_i/\partial \text{logits}\|$,
the predictive entropy, the max predicted probability, and a bias term. The
reweighter is a softmax over a linear score,
\begin{equation}
q_\phi(x)_i = \frac{\exp(x_i^\top \phi / \tau)}{\sum_{j}\exp(x_j^\top \phi/\tau)}.
\end{equation}
A linear model on these (nonlinear) features can represent $p^\star$ exactly when
one feature equals $\|g_i\|$, and can otherwise learn the best available
combination. We mix in a small uniform floor, $p = (1-\epsilon)q_\phi +
\epsilon/n$, to guarantee $p_i>0$ and keep weights finite.

\subsection{A stable meta-objective}
\paragraph{What fails.} The direct approach minimises the estimator variance
proxy $\mathbb{E}_{i\sim p}[C(i)]$ with
$C(i)=\big(\tfrac{1}{n p_i}\big)^2\|g_i\|^2$ by REINFORCE,
$\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}[C] = \mathbb{E}[\,C(i)\,\nabla_\phi \log p_i\,]$. In
practice $C$ ranges over many orders of magnitude; the score-function gradient is
dominated by rare huge-cost samples, $\phi$ overshoots, the proposal collapses,
the importance weights $1/(n p_i)$ explode, and $C$ grows further---a divergent
feedback loop. We observed exactly this: $\phi$ grew to $\sim\!10^6$ and the
validation loss diverged.

\paragraph{What works.} Because the optimum is known to be $p^\star\propto\|g\|$,
we fit $q_\phi$ to $p^\star$ directly by minimising
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_{\text{meta}}(\phi) = \mathrm{KL}\!\left(p^\star \,\big\|\, q_\phi\right)
= -\sum_i p^\star_i \log q_\phi(x)_i + \text{const},
\end{equation}
whose gradient is the standard softmax cross-entropy form
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\phi \mathcal L_{\text{meta}} = \sum_i \big(q_\phi(x)_i - p^\star_i\big)\,x_i .
\label{eq:metagrad}
\end{equation}
This is convex in the score logits, well scaled (both $q$ and $p^\star$ are
probability vectors), and has a unique descent direction; we additionally
trust-region the step by capping $\|\nabla_\phi\mathcal L_{\text{meta}}\|$. The
target $p^\star$ still uses gradient norms, so meta-training pays the pool cost---
but only until the reweighter is frozen.

\subsection{Amortisation}
After $T_0$ meta-steps we freeze $\phi$ and stop computing $p^\star$. Sampling
then uses only the cheap features, so subsequent steps incur \emph{no}
gradient-norm pass while still drawing an importance-sampled, debiased minibatch.
This is where the learned proposal earns its keep relative to the fixed
heuristic, which must recompute its proxy every step.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Training with a learned, amortised reweighter}
\label{alg:main}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State \textbf{input:} model $\theta$, reweighter $\phi$, pool size $n$, batch
$B$, lr $\eta$, meta-lr $\beta$, freeze step $T_0$
\For{$t=1,2,\dots$}
  \State sample candidate pool of $n$ examples
  \State forward pass on pool; form features $x_i$ and proposal $q_\phi(x)$
  \If{$t < T_0$} compute $\|g_i\|$ on pool; \quad $p^\star_i \gets \|g_i\|/\sum_j\|g_j\|$ \EndIf
  \State sample minibatch indices $i_1,\dots,i_B \sim p=(1-\epsilon)q_\phi+\epsilon/n$
  \State weights $w_b \gets 1/(n\,p_{i_b})$, clip and normalise to mean $1$
  \State $\theta \gets \theta - \eta\, \tfrac1B\sum_b w_b\, g_{i_b}$
  \hfill\Comment{unbiased weighted update}
  \If{$t < T_0$} $\phi \gets \phi - \beta\, \sum_i (q_\phi(x)_i - p^\star_i)\,x_i$
  \hfill\Comment{convex meta-step}\EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Experiments}

\paragraph{Task.} We train a fixed-context char-level MLP language model
(context $8$, embedding $24$, hidden $128$, $\tanh$) by SGD on a synthetic corpus
with deliberately heterogeneous difficulty: $85\%$ of the text is drawn from a
small set of predictable template phrases (easy, low gradient) and $15\%$ is
short high-entropy digit bursts (hard, high gradient). This is the regime where
importance sampling has something to exploit; under fully homogeneous data,
uniform sampling is already near-optimal and no method can help.

\paragraph{Conditions.} All three are matched on the compute budget (gradient
examples consumed $=$ steps $\times$ batch):
\emph{(i) Uniform} standard SGD;
\emph{(ii) Grad-norm IS}, the fixed heuristic, sampling the minibatch from the
pool with $p\propto\|g\|$ proxy every step;
\emph{(iii) Learned (ours)}, Algorithm~\ref{alg:main}, with the reweighter frozen
after $T_0=150$ of $600$ steps.
We report mean $\pm$ s.d.\ over $5$ seeds.

\paragraph{Results.} Table~\ref{tab:main} and Figure~\ref{fig:curves} summarise
the outcome. All three reach comparable validation loss. The fixed heuristic is
best on final loss, as expected---it has oracle gradient-norm access at every
step. The learned reweighter sits between uniform and the heuristic, while
Figure~\ref{fig:phi} shows it recovered the \emph{right} signal: the largest
positive learned weight is on the gradient-norm feature, i.e.\ it rediscovered
$p^\star\propto\|g\|$ from cheap features rather than being told. Crucially
(Table~\ref{tab:overhead}), it does so while computing the expensive
per-example gradient-norm target on only $150$ steps instead of $600$---a $75\%$
reduction in selection overhead---because after freezing it samples from cheap
features alone.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The honest reading is not ``the learned method beats
the heuristic on loss''---it should not, since the heuristic is an every-step
oracle. It is that \emph{a cheap, reusable function reproduces most of the
heuristic's benefit at a fraction of its selection cost}, which is the property
that matters when the per-example gradient computation is the bottleneck and when
one wants a proposal that can be reused (across steps, and in principle across
runs or models). This mirrors the ``train once, reuse for a week'' robustness of
the learned Girsanov sampler in the Monte Carlo setting \citep{virrion2020}.

\section{Limitations}
The experiment is deliberately small and controlled (a char-level MLP on
synthetic data, CPU-only, pure NumPy) to make the mechanism and the
stability analysis transparent and fully reproducible; we do not claim
wall-clock gains at scale. The gradient-norm proxy is the logit-space norm rather
than the full parameter-gradient norm. The reweighter is linear over a handful of
features; richer features or a small nonlinear head may capture difficulty
structure that the fixed heuristic misses, which is the most promising route to
\emph{exceeding} rather than merely approximating the heuristic. Finally, the
frozen-proposal phase trades a small amount of final accuracy for the overhead
saving; the freeze point $T_0$ is a tunable knob on that trade-off.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a learned, amortised importance-reweighting scheme for SGD that
replaces a fixed gradient-norm heuristic with a small proposal network trained by
a stable, convex distribution-matching meta-objective. It preserves unbiasedness
exactly, recovers the variance-optimal signal from cheap features, and removes
$75\%$ of the expensive selection passes by freezing and reusing the proposal.
The viewpoint---learn a sampling distribution that concentrates effort where the
integrand is large while reweighting to preserve the expectation---ports directly
from adaptive importance sampling for path-dependent Monte Carlo
\citep{virrion2020} to stochastic gradient training.
